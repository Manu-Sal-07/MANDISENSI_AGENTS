\chapter*{\centering Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
Vehicular occlusion remains a critical challenge in intelligent transportation systems, particularly in complex urban environments where objects are frequently partially or fully hidden from view.In this project, we propose a deep learning-based comprehensive pipeline for the accurate detection and mitigation of occlusions by implementing multiple state-of-the-art models. It uses YOLOv11n to detect and Mask2Former to segment instances, MiDaS to estimate depth and DeepSORT to track several objects and predict motion.  The dataset used to train and evaluate the code was the BDD100K. This dataset was selected owing to its size and variety. The BDD100K dataset used synthetic occlusions and augmentation to simulate real-world traffic conditions. Based on the findings, there and tracking and detection of partially closed vehicles get better in the ability to track and detection. Confidence when it comes to detection is above 80 percent in challenging detection scenarios. This work contributes to the11th goal on SDG, on sustainable cities, which aims to improve user safety, reliability, and efficiency in urban mobility. The system can be incorporated in the autonomous vehicles and traffic data analytics in smart cities.

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Background}
The world of transport is changing quickly. Thanks to smart vehicles, advanced traffic systems, and wide-area video surveillance, the roads are foremost, safer, smarter, and more connected than ever. However, one difficult problem still remains. Occlusion is still on difficult problem. This is when one object closes the view of another, thus hiding the pedestrian or vehicle. Although it was discussed previously, the issue has gained attention along with the automated systems that need to make rapid and precise decisions on the road \cite{paper1}. City landscapes present a unique challenge to the complexities of vehicle and pedestrian traffic, as well as cyclist movement. Important objects can be occluded behind traffic signs, parked cars, and even trees. Humans can supplement their decisions with intuition when driving, while computers make errors when there is missing data. Modern vehicles come equipped with a variety of technologies including cameras, sensors and AI. Such technologies assist in making roads safer by managing occlusions in the road ahead. Research has shown that occlusions are responsible for a large percentage of misses in automated driving systems. The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration has shown that a significant percentage of road collisions are unmarked by the human driver or sensor and are therefore missed. The need for systems that can identify occluded objects only grows as urban environments become more populated. A smart vehicle does not mean that it only has the capability to identify items within the field of view of other vehicles. It can identify occluded objects using sophisticated algorithms and fusion of data from various vehicles and sensor combinations. Autonomous vehicles promise positive and negative contributions to society. These cars should sense and anticipate the movements of surrounding objects in real time.  To accomplish this, the cars need to use and learn over time from cameras, lidar, radar, and other sensors. Overcoming occlusion is not just a technical issue, but fundamental to the need for trust in autonomous systems and for safety in a broader sense. 

Public safety and urban management are also affected by occlusion. In smart city traffic management, occluded vehicles or pedestrians result in missed detection at intersections, which in turn cause delays and accidents.  It is also frustrating for insurance companies when a video fails to show all the vehicles for a car-on-car collision. Analysis of insurance claims and post-event investigations are among the most pressing and beneficial for society applications of occlusion-aware detection. If incident video footage is available, claims can be settled fairly and quickly by revealing hidden vehicles or objects. This also aids law enforcement in incident reconstruction. To analyze near misses and accidents, logistics and fleet operators review dashcam footage, even when large portions are occluded.  Public transport authorities hope to equip buses and trams to detect occluded pedestrians or vehicles. Without safety-critical applications, the world is beginning to appreciate occlusion-aware perception. City officials rely on computer vision systems for understanding and measuring vehicular traffic and pedestrian traffic within a given area, and analyzing and tracking how different public infrastructure elements are used over time. There are gaps within the data, however, in circumstances when there are unaccounted for obstructions, e.g. when there are people walking behind parked vehicles and or cyclists positioned behind large vehicles. There are systems that have been incorporated within traffic signal control, road design, pedestrian signal placements and emergency service dispatch protocols that are driven by this data. Cities are able to enhance their planning and policy making when the data from the cities are reliable.

Some autonomous systems that exemplify the need for state-of-the-art detection are self-driving cars, delivery robots and drones. Uncertainty configurations arise when an object or a person is occluded in the scene and this influences the perception pipeline. This uncertainty directly influences the predictors in the system. Advanced predictors in such systems would improve navigation in the presence of unencountered, unforecasted occlusions, increased navigation on high risk pathways and compliance with safety standards. The need for systems that handle occlusions with high reliability is clearly illustrated with the advent of high density urban environments and the deployment of autonomous systems. Monitors and surveillance systems that incorporate automated detection event systems are still encountering problems with occlusions. In locations, such as airports, railway stations, public events, etc., there is heavy crowding due to which individuals often obstruct each other’s views to the camera. Tracking, behavior recognition, and anomaly detection systems cannot work well under these conditions. Predicting the missing parts' position, or completing the missing parts via body reconstruction for the purposes of improving situational awareness, are also valid methods for such risky situations. This enables the law enforcement and emergency management teams to act in an informed manner and make decisions quickly. Given the numerous domains where the ability to infer, reconstruct predict occluded objects is valuable, this is a positive development. With a better visual emphasis on the objects within view, detection, especially occlusion sensitive detection, can enhance the safety of urban environments, and transportation systems. This can facilitate automated mobility, and enhance public safety and security. This is what makes it a critical component of the Coming advancements in visual reasoning systems. The applications of robust occlusion detection are broad, but the main focus of this project is:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Insurance Claim Analysis:} 
    Fairness and accuracy in evaluating insurance claims is one of the areas where occlusion-aware detection can bring the most benefits. A vehicle or an object can be entirely occluded or partially occluded by a pole, another vehicle, and environmental clutter. This is a common occurrence in most accident scenarios. In conventional video analysis, these occlusions can be missed, as a result creating a liability gap or an incomplete optical estimate of the scene. This problematic scenario is commonly seen in accident scenarios.
    \textit{Example:} In a case of a three-car pile-up at an intersection, suppose Car A has a dashcam, and, in all the video footage, only Car B is visible, while Car C is completely hidden by a truck. Models that account for occlusion can reconstruct or infer the presence of Car C, enabling the insurance company to know all the parties involved and accurately assess the claim.

    \item \textbf{Post-Event Investigation:} 
    Detecting occlusion can help make the insurance claim process more equitable. When an accident occurs, a car or other object may be partially or completely hidden behind another vehicle, a pole, or another object that is present in the environment at the time of the accident. In such cases, video evidence analysis may not pinpoint the essential elements that clarify the accident and, thus, liability may remain in dispute.
In the case of a pile-up of three vehicles at an intersection, the three vehicles may be in completely different orientations. 
    \textit{Example:} In the case of a dispute of Car A versus Car B, Car A’s dashcam may only reveal Car B, while, in all the footage, Car C is completely occluded behind a truck. Systems that account for occlusion can accurately reconstruct or infer the presence of Car C and thus equip the insurer with claim-relevant data concerning all parties involved.

    \item \textbf{Smart City Traffic Management:} 
    The systems used for managing traffic in cities are often obstructed due to large vehicles, buildings, and pedestrians. These beg blind detections, which, in turn, leads to poor traffic-light optimizing, dangerous configurations (over due for a safety measure?), and incorrect estimates of congestion. Integrating occlusion-aware detection with the city-wide camera systems, all road users will remain in view.
\textit{Example:} For instance, a bus stopping at a busy intersection could block a view of a cyclist sitting on the left. An occlusion-aware driver assistance system is one that is able to sense the cyclist in bypassing sight. Such information can be used by traffic signal systems designed to adapt and respond to cyclists to ensure safe system operation and collision avoidance.

    \item \textbf{Fleet Safety Monitoring:} 
Most players of the industry are heavily relying on dashcams to analyze and review near-misses and behaviors of the drivers involved in an incident. Dashcams are prone to recording occlusions and that means the recording could skip over important elements of the incident, for example people who are parked on the side of the street and who may step into the street at an inopportune time. Safety managers need occlusion detections to mitigate the risk of the lesser visible dangers.
\textit{Example:} A truck just barely misses a pedestrian who is occluded behind a side street stall (which won't be visible on a recording without occlusion sensing, and thus greatly inhibits risk assessment and improvement of safety).

    \item \textbf{Autonomous Vehicle R\&D (Secondary):} 
For most players in the logistics industry, driver behaviour analysis, incident review and near-miss are only possible through dashcam recordings. Video occlusion in dashcams can hide important details like pedestrians coming out from behind parked vehicles and motorcyclists cruising in from blind spots. Doing occlusion detects that closed the gap in safety managers finding these hidden risks.
\textit{Example:} While testing an autonomous vehicle, it proceeds to an intersection, where a truck causes a child on the road to become momentarily obstructed from view. Using an occlusion-aware algorithm, the vehicle can detect that there is likely a child around, and it will proactively slow down.

    \item \textbf{Public Transport Safety:} 
Buses, streetcars, and other large vehicles are plagued by blind spots, especially around stops and other crowded environments. There is always an increased risk of collisions because pedestrians or vehicles can suddenly become occluded. Detection systems that account for occlusion can assist and warn people or autonomous systems that are operating the vehicle of possible threats.
\textit{Example:} A driver’s side mirror will not see a pedestrian behind the shelter at the bus stop. Using occlusion-aware sensor fusion, systems can warn drivers of pedestrians before the vehicle is driven out from the bus stop.
\end{itemize}

Put plainly, occlusion is real and impactful. This project seeks to cover a part of the industry that needs immediate attention and at the same time improves safety for self-driving vehicles by focusing on insurance claim investigations and incident reviews.

\section{Problem Statement}

\subsection{The Challenge of Occlusion in Traffic Analysis}
The main problem examined in this research is the huge impact occlusion has on accident reconstruction and insurance claims analysis. Following traffic accidents, reconstruction is often not possible when crucial information is unavailable. The issue occurs most prominently in a complicated urban environment, where multiple vehicles, pedestrians, and environmental elements interact. Moreover, these interactions often obscure important details from dashcams and surveillance systems.

\subsection{Impact on Insurance Claim Processing}
Claims that have special visual evidence that is obscured pose significant difficulties for the insurance sector. According to recent estimates, about 30\% of traffic accidents happen due to some lack of visibility.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Cases with occluded proof have had an average delay in claim processing times of 45%.
    \item Claims were disputed at a higher rate (25\% more for claims with high obstruction).
    \item Increased operating costs as a result of manual review requirements.
    \item Inconsistent claim resolutions across similar cases.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations of Current Approaches}
This low level of occlusion is challenging for traditional methods during post-event analysis.

\subsection{Technological Limitations}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Single-camera systems cannot perceive depth nor correlate angles.
    \item Basic motion detection won't work if the object is partly or fully obscured.
    \item Current algorithms often misinterpret occluded objects as separate objects.
    \item They see something only because there is someone else there.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Procedural Challenges}
\begin{itemize}
    \item We rely on manual reviews that take a long time and can make mistakes.
    \item No set-of-standards for handling occluded evidence in a courtroom.
    \item Trainers are having difficulty teaching staff how to record anything occlusion related.
    \item Occlusion analysis application is not the same for all insurance.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Secondary Challenge: Autonomous Vehicle Integration}
Even though the main aim is post-event analysis, such developed solutions would also help in forwarding the autonomous vehicle technology. The ability to handle occlusion is crucial for.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Making quick choices in tough traffic situations better.
    \item Improving prediction skills to find hidden objects.
    \item Make safety rules better for robot systems.
    \item Making improved training datasets for machine learning models.
\end{itemize}

\section{Objectives}
The main objective of this project is to enhance detection of vehicles, as well as other objects, which are either partially or fully occluded. Both in driving will help and also in analyzing the event after it is done. The objectives are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The goal is to design algorithms that can identify vehicles even partially obscured.
    \item Take information from different video frames to boost detection during blockage.
    \item To assess the system performance under varying traffic conditions and environments.
    \item To compare our system with existing occlusion detection and mitigation methods..
    \item This will be used later for integration with autonomous vehicles, smart traffic, etc.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Research Objectives}
Through the following objectives, this work intends to tackle these problems.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Create algorithms for computer vision that can identify hidden vehicles in traffic footage and track them.
    \item Create a framework for reconstructing occluded elements using multi-source data fusion.
    \item Set criteria to evaluate whether occlusion analyses are accurate enough for legal and insurance purposes.
    \item Offer instruments to improve post-event analysis accuracy and efficiency.
    \item Help create industry standards for dealing with obstructed evidence. 
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Expected Impact}
The success of this research will yield this.
\begin{itemize}
    \item More accurate and efficient insurance claim processing.
    \item Reduced investigation times for traffic incidents.
    \item Greater consistency in legal outcomes.
    \item It makes driving safer for humans and automated systems.
    \item Law enforcement and insurance companies can allocate resources better. 
\end{itemize}
This research to a great extent deals to solve one of the most challenging aspects of traffic analysis and accident reconstruction in the insurance and legal sectors and future transportation technology.

\subsection{Why This Matters}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{When occlusion is handled better, there are less accidents.}  
    When they accurately detect pedestrians and cyclists whose upper body is hidden behind the vehicle ahead, as well as vehicles whose front is concealed behind an obstacle, it reduces the likelihood of missed warnings or delayed responses. Accident risks get cut down in cities and highways and it also gives a better performance and safer intersections of the vehicle.

    \item \textbf{After the momentous event, the investigation can be easily conducted by the responsible authorities and insurance companies.}  
    The occlusion-aware analysis does not overlook important information like hidden vehicles, obstructed paths, or blocked view. By putting together the pieces of the puzzle, investigators can get a better understanding of the event, who is responsible for it, and the settlement of a claim or even a dispute in court.

    \item \textbf{The public starts accepting and trusting autonomous vehicles more.}  
    One of the hardest things for an autonomous car to pull off is to navigate safely in a scenario where one of the objects is blocked from view.

\section{Related Work on Vehicle Occlusion Detection}
It has been a long-standing challenge to solve vehicle occlusion in computer vision. Traffic surveillance, autonomous driving and intelligent transportation systems applications will all benefit. The original research often followed the classical vision pipelines. Deep learning architectures now allow researchers to reason about partially visible objects. This section summarizes the major categories of related work. It also discusses their mathematical foundations. Finally, it also describes their strengths and weaknesses.  Representative studies are cited accordingly. Occupancy detection of traffic scenes was mostly done with increased geometric reasoning and motion analysis usage. The trend of doing handcrafted feature extraction also increased. The commonly used techniques include background subtraction and optical flow among others. Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) were commonly used for estimating foreground masks. However, occlusion disrupted the motion of the complete pixels, resulting in fragmentation or merging of object regions. A partial contour matching method tried to reconstruct the visible boundaries of vehicles using parametric shape models. In classical tracking systems, the Kalman Filters (KF) and its variants were applied to estimating the object states. In the case of occlusion, the Kalman prediction step occurs as shown in \ref{eq:eq2}) 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:eq2}
\hat{x}_{k|k-1} = A \hat{x}_{k-1|k-1} + Bu_k
\end{equation}
It was used to maintain continuity, and in this case, discontinuous measurements are missing. These models, however, assume linear movements and the noise in the measurements is Gaussian, which does not carry well the more complex the traffic is. More sophisticated versions that employ the Particle Filter (PF) sampled more than one hypothesis of the motion to be able to get around the non-linearities and were able to track quite convincingly even in the case of partial occlusion and tracking. However, classical methods still encountered a lot of trouble in the case of heavy occlusion. Detection of shapes using color histograms, or, say, HOG features were known to work when only a small part of the object was visible. As very thorough reviews, most notably that of Tian et al \cite{paper17}, dense traffic environments are not well-suited for the use of handcrafted features due to the presence of interactions and occlusions.

\subsection{Deep Learning-Based Methods}
 The rise of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) changed how the problem of occlusion detection and recovery was approached. Learning feature representation was the next step, and region based detectors like Faster R-CNN and SSD introduced the use of anchor-box mechanisms, learning occlusion invariant patterns, and being able to detect partially visible vehicles. For CNN-based detectors, treating occlusion as a natural variation within the data distribution shi fted generalization to a different level compared to traditional models. More recent approaches to occlusion detection and recovery utilize integrations of attention mechanisms, deformable convolutions, and/or multiscale feature fusion. Particularly, feature pyramid networks (FPNs) have become popular for being able to detect small and/or partially occluded objects by merging low-level spatial resolution and high-level semantic features. Further along, transformer-based architectures have built upon these improvements, making the unwarte of occlusion even more robust by modeling long-dependencies. Attention mechanisms have also shown improvements with respect to object continuity even for studies like Wang et al ~\cite{paper26}, where the surface of the object of interest, in this case the vehicle, was highly occluded. Let $f_\theta(I)$ be a function that maps from the image $I$ to the feature space. The objective is to maximize detection confidence even when only a subset of pixel evidence is available. This is achieved by minimizing a loss function such as \ref{eq:eq3}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:eq3}
    \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{cls} + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{reg}
\end{equation}

where $\mathcal{L}_{cls}$ accounts for classification under occlusion, and $\mathcal{L}_{reg}$ ensures stable bounding-box localization even when object boundaries are ambiguous.

\subsection{Multi-Object Tracking and Occlusion Reasoning}
Occlusion is very important in multi-object tracking (MOT). In tracking-by-detection frameworks, trackers predict object trajectories by associating detections across frames. But when occlusion happens, the detection disappears and the tracker can only rely on motion models or appearance embeddings. Approaches like these are SORT and DeepSORT, where the latter uses a deep embedding network in order to keep identity consistency despite short-term occlusions. More recent works have modelled occlusion more explicitly thanks to GNNs, or even a trajectory forecasting model. An example is Zhang et al.~\cite{paper22} who propose a method to infer occlusions based on a pairwise visibility constraint between the vehicles. These methods assume vehicles have realistic motion dynamics and occlusion patterns can be predicted using contextual cues from nearby objects. The overall multi-object tracking and occlusion loss was defined as in Equation~\ref{eq:mot_occlusion}, where the association loss and occlusion loss are balanced through a weighting factor.

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mot_occlusion}
    \mathcal{L}_{mot} = \mathcal{L}_{assoc} + \lambda_{occ} \mathcal{L}_{occ}
\end{equation}

\subsection{3D and Sensor-Fusion Approaches}
LiDAR, radar, and multi-camera systems serve to mitigate the challenges posed by single-vision occlusions in the context of fully autonomous driving. 3D bounding-box prediction networks like PointRCNN and BEV (Bird’s Eye View) detectors calculate the parameters of an object, even those which might be occluded by the vehicle, from a given vantage point. Depth improves reasoning with occlusions by enabling the separation of foreground from the background. RGB image representations occluded by background are enhanced owing to the depth and/or LiDAR fusion image of them in more advanced approaches. Liu et al.~\cite{paper31} demonstrate the completion of the partially occluded vehicle 3D shape in the feature space in the context of cross-modal attention. These techniques are necessary and efficient in full-scale urban environments with obstructed lines of sight. The sensor fusion approaches combined loss, considering the balance of RGB and depth, is defined in Equation~\ref{eq:3d_fusion} below.



\begin{equation}
\label{eq:3d_fusion}
    \mathcal{L}_{fusion} = \mathcal{L}_{rgb} + \lambda_{depth} \mathcal{L}_{depth}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Generative and Reconstruction-Based Approaches}
The latest generation of methods aims not just at detecting the occluded object, but also at reconstructing it. Image generation techniques using GANs, VAEs, or diffusion models attempt to hallucinate or predict the hidden parts of the vehicle. These models are trained to predict possible shapes or textures that could fill the occluded parts. It has been shown that the generative completion of occluded vehicles improves their subsequent detection and trajectory estimation. Let $I_v$ be the visible part and $I_h$ be the occluded part. As shown in papers.~\cite{paper33,paper14}enerative occlusion recovery attempts to model:
\begin{equation}
p(I_h \mid I_v)
\end{equation}
which yields a learned reconstruction of the hidden content. Although these methods show promise, they require large annotated datasets and careful regularization to avoid unrealistic predictions.

\subsection{Background Subtraction (e.g., GSOM)}
Background subtraction identifies moving objects by modeling the scene background over time. For each pixel $x$, the background model $B(x, t)$ is updated as shown in equation~\ref{eq:eq1}:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:eq1}
    B(x, t) = \alpha I(x, t) + (1-\alpha) B(x, t-1)
\end{equation}

where $I(x, t)$ is the observed intensity and $\alpha$ is the learning rate. This method is also very fast and simple, but it has issues with overlapping/occluded objects and dynamic backgrounds, which is because it cannot differentiate between several moving objects. This is explained in detail in the paper ~\cite{paper8}. Modern background subtraction techniques, like GMMs and GSOM, increase robustness to the changes in the background by modeling each pixel with several distributions or adaptive prototypes. These methods are better at dealing with gradual illumination changes, repetitive motion (e.g., waving trees), and shadows. It is worth mentioning, however, that not all scenarios are smooth sailing. These methods have problems with rapid illumination changes, camera jitter, and persistent backgrounds that absorb into the background model. A variety of applications can be developed based on background subtraction.

\subsection{Kalman Filter}
The Kalman Filter is a type of recursive estimator that you can refresh and use again. It estimates the following state and updates it based on new measurements:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x}_k = A \mathbf{x}_{k-1} + B \mathbf{u}_k + \mathbf{w}_k
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}_k = H \mathbf{x}_k + \mathbf{v}_k
\end{equation}
The state transition matrix $A$ is the control input, the observation matrix $B$, the process stochastic $H$, and the measurement stochastic $\mathbf{w}_k$,$\mathbf{v}_k$.
If an object is concealed they filter has faulty predictions. This could cause drift from an object’s motion over time or an object moving in an erratic fashion.  

As described in paper ~\cite{44}, a Kalman filter incorporates two mechanisms; prediction and updating. Discard and lose information pertaining to the recent past, the filter predicts the object’s state and associated uncertainty for the desired time. As of the most recent observation, filter matrix is formed, and the matrix is modified based on the biases of the estimated state and the measurement. In a real world situation, the Kalman filter a sample of the state continuous system in motion to provide an estimation of the state of a system in motion in real time. Kalman Filters have a wide variety of applications in tracking and navigation. In addition, they are very useful in sensor fusion employed in robotics or self-driving cars. However, If the system is non-linear or if the noise has a non-Gaussian distribution, the system will be inefficient. During periods of occlusion, the imaginary state can divert to a incorrect path and trigger a loss of a true trace or an identity switch.

The EKF and UKF have been created to address non-linear issues. Motion restrictions or multiple hypothesis tracking will add occlusion strength for features of additional appearance. In real-time applications, trackers, so-called Kalman Filters, are often combined with detection-based associations, in order to maintain accurate tracking within congested or visually complex scenes.

\subsection{Particle Filter}
Sequential Monte Carlo or particle filters employ a weighted sample to construct a proxy of the probability distribution for the state of the object as shown in Eq. ~\ref{eq:pf_samples}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pf_samples}
\{x_k^{(i)}, w_k^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^N
\end{equation}
Here, the particle at moment $k$'s position in time is denoted $x_k^{(i)}$, with $w_k^{(i)}$ representing the particle's weight. In paper ~\cite{39} of the paper, the quantity of particles as a group is said to reflect the posterior distribution. Therefore, in association with a Bayesian filter, this filter functions to encapsulate uncertainty in the form of multiple hypotheses to represent difference states of the object. For every iteration, particles are spread with the help of a motion model, and their weights get updated according to measurement probability. This \textit{e}- \textit{p} algorithm allows the filter to maintain at least one hypothesis when a target is momentarily occluded or when the target exhibits extreme non-linear motion. On the other hand, these particle filters are made to operate with a very large quantity of particles in order to conduct a more accurate estimation. If there is a complete block, all of the weight will collapse, which may cause the filter to degenerate. Therefore it will need some form of resampling. or some regularization techniques.

\subsection{Optical Flow}
Optical flow makes an estimate of the motion between two image frames. The energy functional described in Equation~\ref{eq:of_energy} is a basis of the classic Horn-Schunck method:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:of_energy}
E = \iint \left[ (I_x u + I_y v + I_t)^2 + \alpha^2 (|\nabla u|^2 + |\nabla v|^2) \right] dx dy
\end{equation}
In this equation, $u$ and $v$ are the horizontal and vertical components of the flow, while $I_x, I_y, I_t$ are the spatial and temporal image gradients. The first term keeps the brightness consistent and the second term makes the rendering smooth By solving Equation~\ref{eq:of_energy}, the algorithm estimates pixel-wise motion, which is valuable for tracking object boundaries and inferring motion even in the presence of partial occlusion. However, optical flow methods can struggle with large displacements, homogeneous regions, or when the occluded region lacks texture, leading to ambiguous or inaccurate flow estimates. Extensions such as pyramidal or deep-learning-based optical flow improve robustness to these issues. These advantages and drawbacks are explained clearly in the "Vehicle Detection using Optical Flow" paper ~\cite{paper48}

\subsection{CNN-based Detection (YOLO, Faster R-CNN)}
In research literature ~\cite{paper1,paper4,paper11,paper19}, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) learn hierarchical features for object detection, as described by the mapping in Equation~\ref{eq:cnn_output}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cnn_output}
\text{Output} = f(\text{Conv}(I; W), b)
\end{equation}
Here, $\text{Conv}(I; W)$ denotes the convolution of input image $I$ with learned weights $W$, and $f$ is a non-linear activation function. Modern detectors like YOLO and Faster R-CNN use such architectures to extract features and directly predict bounding boxes and class probabilities. The loss function, shown in Equation~\ref{eq:cnn_loss}, typically combines localization, confidence, and classification terms:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cnn_loss}
\text{min} \sum_{i=1}^S \sum_{j=1}^B \mathbb{1}_{ij}^{obj} (x_i - \hat{x}_i)^2 + ...
\end{equation}
This formulation encourages the network to accurately localize and classify objects, even when they are partially occluded. By training on diverse datasets with occlusion scenarios, CNNs can learn to infer missing parts. However, as Equation~\ref{eq:cnn_loss} suggests, severe occlusion or lack of training data can still lead to missed detections or false positives. 

\subsection{Attention Mechanisms}
Attention mechanism assigns weights to different sections in space or time, so that the model can learn to focus on the right things. Equation ~\ref{eq:attn} describes the essential operation:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:attn}
\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V
\end{equation}
In this equation, query, key, and value matrix are represented by $Q$, $K$, $V$ respectively. $d_k$ stands for the dimension of the key. The softmax weights determine the relevance of each region or token. By explicitly computing attention as in Equation~\ref{eq:attn}, the model can prioritize visible or informative areas and downweight occluded or irrelevant regions. This mechanism is particularly effective in transformer-based architectures, where global dependencies are captured. Attention improves robustness to occlusion by enabling the network to aggregate cues from non-occluded regions, but its effectiveness depends on the diversity and quality of training data. These are explained in the paper ~\cite{paper33}.

\subsection{Transformer-based Models}
Transformers use self-attention to model global dependencies in images or sequences. The Vision Transformer (ViT) splits images into patches and processes them as tokens, applying self-attention as formulated in Equation~\ref{eq:selfattn}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:selfattn}
\text{Self-Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V
\end{equation}
Equation~\ref{eq:selfattn} enables the model to relate information from all patches, allowing aggregation of context from non-occluded regions to infer occluded content. This global reasoning is a key advantage of transformers over local convolutional models. However, transformers require large datasets and significant computational resources for effective training. When properly trained, they can infer missing object parts and maintain performance under occlusion, but may still struggle with rare or extreme occlusion cases not seen during training. This method is used in and explained by papers ~\cite{paper10,paper42,paper47}

\subsection{3D Object Detection}
3D detectors (e.g., PointPillars, PV-RCNN) use LiDAR or stereo vision to estimate object position in 3D space. The general formulation is given in Equation~\ref{eq:3d_obj}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:3d_obj}
\text{Object Position} = f(\text{PointCloud}, \text{Image})
\end{equation}
Here, the function $f$ combines information from point clouds and images to predict the 3D location and orientation of objects. In differentiating layers of foreground and background, 3D detectors and analyses can differentiate layers and remove occlusions. However, these methods require specialized. expensive 3D sensors, LiDAR, and are sensitive to missing or noisy data or data point errors. Also, these techniques do 3D analyses, which are more computational expensive than 2D analyses, which do 2D analyses. Therefore, or it is an active research topics, is 3D analyses for robotics and autonomous vehicles, especially in for 3D object point detections, 3D and 2D planes for dense situations or cluttered and occluded ,e.g. vehicles, areas. 3D labeled object detections are moreed used in vehicles and is explored in papers ~\cite{paper2,paper15,paper16,paper23,paper39,paper46,paper50}


\subsection{Multi-Camera Fusion}
Multi-camera systems combine views to reduce occlusion by providing complementary perspectives. Fusion can be performed at the feature or decision level, as described by Equation~\ref{eq:mc_fusion}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mc_fusion}
\mathbf{z}_{\text{fused}} = F(\mathbf{z}_1, \mathbf{z}_2, ..., \mathbf{z}_N)
\end{equation}
In Equation~\ref{eq:mc_fusion}, $F$ denotes the fusion function (e.g., averaging, concatenation, or learned weights), and $\mathbf{z}_i$ are the features or decisions from each camera. This approach enhances visibility and reduces the impact of occlusion, as objects hidden in one view may be visible in another. However, effective fusion requires precise calibration and synchronization of cameras, and misalignment can introduce artifacts. Multi-camera fusion is widely used in intelligent transportation and surveillance systems, especially in complex urban environments. Referenced by papers \cite{paper38,paper41, paper46}

\subsection{DeepSORT Tracking}
The appearance features extracted from a CNN along with the Kalman filter motion are utilized for tracking by DeepSORT. The association cost for matching detections to tracks is defined in Equation~\ref{eq:ds_cost}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ds_cost}
\text{Association cost} = \lambda \cdot \text{Mahalanobis}(\text{motion}) + (1-\lambda) \cdot \text{cosine}(\text{appearance})
\end{equation}
The Mahalanobis distance measures motion similarity while cosine distance measures appearance similarity. This value of $\lambda$ is determining the weight of score. So the objects that were briefly out of view due to occlusion can be tracked. However, if the identity, or tracked the object, lost due to significant occlusions, and to track, the two terms of the score ~\ref{eq:ds_cost} becomes stagnant, and an object identity switch can occur. To facilitate this, it is suggested to enhance the track score and track set such that one class of the tracked objects is improved in loss of track. This method is detailed in papers ~\cite{paper20, paper30,paper35} 

\subsection{V2X Fusion}
The concept of Vehicle-to-Everything (V2X) is centered on providing perception enhancement through the integration of the vehicles, the infrastructure, and the sensor data from pedestrians while eliminating occlusions. Equation ~\ref{eq:v2x_fusion} describes the weighted sum representation of the fusion process:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:v2x_fusion}
\mathbf{z}_{\text{fused}} = \sum_{i=1}^N w_i \mathbf{z}_i
\end{equation}
The symbol system represents the values related to node sensor measurements and the associated weights. In this case, each node (whether it is a vehicle, infrastructure, or a pedestrian) has sensor measurements denoted by $\mathbf{z}_i$. The weights $w_i$ indicate the degree to which a particular measurement is considered dependable or pertinent. Equation ~\ref{eq:v2x_fusion} states that the system is able to “see” around occluded objects if it has at least one node that is unobstructed. This ability is crucial in use cases related to driving and safety in a connected environment. However, the V2X fusion is dependent on the presence of supporting infrastructure, effective communication protocols, protective measures for communication to preserve low latency and integrity, and still poses scalability and privacy challenges which are well documented. ~\cite{paper51}.

\section{Machine Learning in Traffic Monitoring}
Thanks to automated object detection and machine learning (ML), smart traffic monitoring is now more advanced than ever. Multi-object tracking, behaviour analysis detection and scene analysis have grown more precise. YOLOv5/v8 and Faster R-CNN have arguably become the most popular and best performing advanced object detectors. The YOLO models are more suited for real-time applications, whereas Faster R-CNN is more accurate, albeit slower. When this model is paired with DeepSORT, which is a tracker that uses a color (appearance) feature model alongside a motion model (Kalman filter), tracking of an object across frames with partial occlusion is attainable. The most sophisticated ML models integrate attention mechanisms and transformers for added complexity and robustness. Self attention mechanisms that focus on certain salient regions of the scene can improve occlusion recovery and small object detection. Meaning there are advanced networks that can better depth estimation and mitigate the limitations of complexity with multi-modal fusion (RGB, LiDAR, or Radar). They can detect overlapping vehicles or multiple pedestrians. The integrating of V2X data can improve detection performance as the system gains information from vehicles and infrastructure sensors. This improves the reduction of occlusion as well as widening the vehicles field of vision in more complex environments.

Proper training and evaluation of ML models are largely contingent on the availability of reliable datasets. Virtual KITTI offers datasets with flawless annotation for evaluating occlusion, while the other two datasets, BDD100K and Cityscapes, contain images from urban traffic scenarios. Improving generalizability of the model can be achieved through data augmentation techniques such as Random Occlusion, Weather Simulation and Scaling.

Beyond these advancements, there are challenges with the use of ML for real-world traffic monitoring as model accuracy is an essential requirement, and models are expected to have low computational cost to ease deployment on edge devices. Domain adaptation and continual learning are other useful techniques to mitigate the challenges posed by changes in lighting, weather, and camera angle. On the whole, the advancements of ML in traffic monitoring systems have improved the reliability and scalability of these systems, making them useful in self-driving vehicles, smart cities, traffic law enforcement, and many other applications.

\section{Limitations of Existing Systems}
Traffic monitoring systems, even with the power they possess, have some major shortcomings:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Partial and Full Occlusion:} Most detection and tracking models are able to cope with partial occlusion—when only part of an object is hidden—however, full occlusion remains a significant challenge in the domain. A pedestrian, for example, walks behind a large vehicle and, unfortunately, goes completely out of view of all cameras. The system thus loses track of their identity concerning the trackers. Therefore, advanced re-identification techniques or trajectory predictions are necessary for recovery.
    \item \textbf{Computing COst:} The transformer models and those using attention mechanisms do not allow for deploying edge devices (semantic segmentation models as well) and may not provide real-time responses, which is needed for safety tasks like collision avoidance or adaptive traffic control.
    \item \textbf{Bias:} Many ML Models are trained on public data that is not representative of their operational environment. There is a tendency to overfitting and a lack of generalization while deploying trained models, as seen with particular cities, weathers and camera classes. If a model has only seen a clear day in Europe, it is not going to function well during a foggy night in Asia.
    \item \textbf{Infrastructure Dep:} AExamples of Advanced systems which rely on V2X or multi-sensor fusion also need tailored hardware such as LiDAR, HD cameras, or roadside units. The costs here are also substantial, and such systems are difficult to implement and manage, especially in developing regions or older systems.
    \item \textbf{Environmental and Adverserial Robustness:} The models used by machine learning can be compromised due to attacks from competitors and environmental changes such as snow, rain and glare on the road etc. The ability to detect and track can degrade, making it unsafe.
    \item \textbf{Privacy and Data Security:} The use of V2X systems has cameras and involves data sharing as well as data privacy and protection issues. Requirements under GDPR and similar regulations lock down data and limit feasibility for large scale systems.
    \item \textbf{Scaling and maintainance:}The rapid expansion of traffic monitoring systems creates difficulties isolating the same performance level data from thousands of cameras and sensors. To avert drift and degradation continuous updates, recalibrations, and monitoring of the systems are needed.
\end{itemize}
In conclusion, the field of traffic monitoring has benefited from the advances of machine learning. However, the challenges above need to be addressed to develop real-world systems that are robust, scalable, and trustworthy.

\subsection*{Overview of Pipeline Stages}
The complete occlusion-aware detection and prediction pipeline consists of eight major stages that allows for accurately interpreting the traffic scene. The next sub-section explains each component in detail.

\begin{enumerate}

    \item \textbf{Input Stage}\\
    The system starts off by taking in traffic videos or image sequences sourced from different places. These include fixed cameras placed at intersections, dashcams from vehicles, or mobile units. The system must be able to deal with different types of video input because these sources might offer video compressed to various frame rate, resolution, and quality. Pre-processing steps can include deinterlacing, denoising, color correction to make standard input. Let equation~\ref{eq:eqc3}
    \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:eqc3}
    V = \{v_t\}_{t=1}^{T}
    \end{equation}
    refer to the arranged collection of video frames. The detection and occlusion reasoning success rate is significantly influenced by the quality, resolution and diversity of the frames in $V$. In real-world applications, input data may be affected by negative weather conditions (rain, fog), low light, or camera vibrations. By and large, the system is designed to confront these problems using adaptive preprocessing and robust modelling training which guarantees trustworthy performance across different wealth environments. You can also associate metadata with each frame, which could be a timestamp, GPS coordinates or camera calibration parameters to support downstream tasks such as trajectory mapping or multi-camera fusion.

    \item \textbf{Frame Extraction}\\
    Through OpenCV or similar libraries, frames are sampled from input video stream either at fixed intervals (eg. every 0.5 seconds) or adaptively depending on the change in the scene. The sampling function $f$ selects frames that maximize information content while minimizing redundancy. We define $f$ to be the sampling function and $S$ to be the selected frames as shown in equation~\ref{eq:eqc32}:
    \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:eqc32}
    S = \{v_t \mid t = k \cdot \Delta t,\; k \in \mathbb{N}\}.
    \end{equation}
    This provides a consistent time distribution and avoids unnecessary processing of redundant frames in long videos. Adaptive frame extraction may use scene change detection, motion analysis, or entropy-based criteria to focus on frames where significant events (e.g., vehicle interactions or occlusions) occur. Extracted frames are indexed and stored for further processing, with associated metadata such as timestamps and source identifiers. In distributed systems, frame extraction can be parallelized or offloaded to edge devices to reduce bandwidth and latency.

    \item \textbf{Object Detection}\\
    YOLOv11n, a state-of-the-art real-time object detector, is used to identify vehicles, pedestrians, and other road users in each frame. The model divides the input image into a grid and, for each cell, predicts bounding boxes, objectness scores, and class probabilities. For grid cell $(i, j)$, the model predicts object probability $p_{ij}$ and bounding box parameters $(x, y, w, h)$. The loss function is formulated as equation~\ref{eq:eqc33}:
    \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:eqc33}
    \text{Loss} = \sum_{i,j} \mathbb{1}_{ij}^{obj} \left[(x_{ij} - \hat{x}_{ij})^2 + (y_{ij} - \hat{y}_{ij})^2 + \cdots \right] 
    + \text{classification loss}.
    \end{equation}
    The output for a frame $t$ is a set of detected objects as equation~\ref{eq:eqc34}:
    \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:eqc34}
    O_t = \{o_{t,1}, o_{t,2}, \ldots\}.
    \end{equation}
    YOLOv11n is chosen for its balance of speed and accuracy, enabling real-time processing even on edge devices. The data augmentation that was used during the training made this step robust for partial occlusions and lighting variations. After the initial detection phase, secondary processes are used to select the best bounding boxes.  Filters out objects that fall under the confidence score threshold to reduce false positives. The detector can be adjusted further to suit different traffic conditions by retraining on local data, so it performs well during night or poor weather.

    \item \textbf{Instance Segmentation}\\
    Mask2Former is used to generate pixel-wise masks for each detected object after detection. Instance segmentation is essential for occlusion and spatial relationship analysis because it provides accurate object boundaries unlike bounding boxes. For the object $o$, we have the predicted segmentation mask as in equation~\ref{eq:eqc35}:
    \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:eqc35}
    M_o = \arg\max_{c}\; P(c \mid \text{features}(o)),
    \end{equation}
    Where $P(c \mid \cdot)$ refers to pixel steady class probabilities for image I The model Mask2Former uses a transformer structure for the collection of global information. Thus it is effective for overlapping and partially visible objects. A mask for each object (binary or multi-class) is produced that can be converted to polygons for geometric analysis. Being able to differentiate between objects, instance segmenting allows one to check relationships between objects as well as measuring various properties of the objects. We can use the segmentation masks to create object specific features (such as color histograms, texture descriptors) for re-identification, tracking etc. In real-world use, we test the segmentation quality against the correct truth to see if it is valid.

    \item \textbf{Depth Estimation}\\
    We use MiDaS, a deep neural network trained on monocular depth prediction, to estimate depth. MiDaS generates a dense depth map $D_t$ for each frame $v_t$, representing the relative distance of each pixel from the camera.  This is formalized as shown in equation~\ref{eq:eqc36}:
    \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:eqc36}
    D_t = f_{\text{depth}}(v_t).
    \end{equation}
    For each segmented object, the mean depth is computed as shown in equation~\ref{eq:eqc37}:
    \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:eqc37}
    \bar{d}_o = \frac{1}{|M_o|} \sum_{(x,y) \in M_o} D_t(x,y),
    \end{equation}
    where $|M_o|$ is the size of the mask $M_o$ in pixels. MiDaS generates an inverted depth map (higher values mean closer objects). All depth maps use a common scale making comparison across frames and objects possible. Depth estimation is essential for telling the difference between foreground and background objects, clearing up the order of occlusions,  and allowing 3D scene reconstruction. In difficult lighting or weather conditions, MiDaS is able to generalize well given its robust training, but one may engage in post processing (e.g., median filtering, outlier removal) for better quality. By having segmentation and depth, the system can conclude which objects are likely to occlude one another and came up with the layout of the scene.

    \item \textbf{Object Tracking}\\
    DeepSORT handles tracking of objects through frames. It uses a Kalman-based motion prediction and appearance Re-Identification (ReID) features. The association cost between detection $i$ and track $j$ is defined as equation~\ref{eq:eqc38}:
    \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:eqc38}
    C(i, j) = \lambda \cdot \text{Mahalanobis}(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) 
            + (1 - \lambda) \cdot (1 - \cos(\phi_i, \phi_j)),
    \end{equation}
    Here, Kalman predicted state is represented by $\mathbf{x}$ and ReID embedded is denoted by $\phi$.
    Kalman updates follow equation~\ref{eq:eqc39}:
    \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:eqc39}
    \mathbf{x}_{k|k} = \mathbf{x}_{k|k-1} + K_k(\mathbf{z}_k - H\mathbf{x}_{k|k-1}),
    \end{equation}
    The tracker gives a unique ID to each object so that they keep their identities even through sporadic occlusions or missed detections. Due to its use of appearance features, DeepSORT is capable of recovering from identity switches when objects are visually similar. The position of an object in a next frame can be calculated using the velocity and acceleration of that object using Kalman’s filter. The ReID network can be used as a robust descriptor for matching appearance. Management of tracks includes initialization, termination, and reactivation of tracks in case objects disappear due to occlusion. To reduce ID switches in high density scenes efficiently, we propose advanced tracklet association and occlusion-aware heuristics.

    \item \textbf{Occlusion Reasoning and Prediction}\\
    The system determines occlusions by analyzing the spatiotemporal relations of the tracked objects and the segmentation masks and depth values. When an object $o$ disappears in frame $t$, we project its future position using its ${\bf v}_{\tau}$ velocity vector. $\mathbf{v}_o$:
    \begin{equation}
    \hat{p}_{t+1} = p_t + \mathbf{v}_o \cdot \Delta t.
    \end{equation}
    Occlusion is inferred when.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item the mask $M_o$ is missing,.
        \item if that overlap exceeds the predicted confidence.
        \item The overlapping object, which is closer, has a lesser depth value.
    \end{itemize}
    The system  produces predicted polygons for likely occluded areas behind foreground objects. This process uses both geometric cues and appearance cues to differentiate between a true occlusions and object exit. False positives from occlusion detection have been reduced through temporal consistency checks and trajectory smoothing. The occlusion regions that are predicted are shown as overlays in the output video along with the stored metadata for downstream analysis. In advanced settings, the machine learning model can analyze past occlusion events and predict the most likely event to occur due to the past system scene context. This enables us to assess risk in advance and carry out activities like autonomous driving and accident analysis.

    \item \textbf{Output Generation}\\
    The pipeline produces a complete set of outputs. These outputs are used for visualization and further analysis. They can also be fed into external systems. These include:
\begin{itemize}
\item We overlay bounding boxes, segmentation masks, shading of depth, tracking ID, and clear occlusion indicators (e.g. polygon colour or dashed outline) onto the processed video. The annotated video frames reveal distinct object trajectories and occlusions. They help a human operator or a downstream algorithm interpret the scenario quickly.
\item For every frame, a JSON file describes the object’s ID, the coordinates of the bounding box, the segmentation masks (in polygons or bit array), the depth statistics, the trajectory, and the occlusion. Automation, auditing, and integration with traffic management or forensic systems are supported by these files. Using a consistent format allows us to analyze many scenes or times at once. 
\item Occlusion-aware trajectory predictions: We can now visualize during occlusions where our system predicts the position and shape of objects! Such predictions are useful for an insurance assessment to reconstruct the accident scenario, traffic safety assessment, and forensic investigation. Capabilities such as interactive dashboards, heat maps of occluded areas, and GIS or CAD output can be included in the advanced output options.
\end{itemize}
The design for the modular output makes it easier to add new formats or downstream applications as per research or operational needs.

\end{enumerate}


\subsection{Data Flow and Module Interaction}
The layout of the data in the pipeline for vehicle occlusion detection for mitigation has been planned for maximum of modularity, transparency, scalability. Every part in the pipe is a user and maker of well-defined data artifacts. As a result, there is easy integration, parallelization, and robust error recovery. This section gives a deep dive of how the data is passed, verified and transformed while it is making its way through the system, as well as the architectural means for deploying the same in the real-world at scale.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Input Getting and Preprocessing}\\
The system accepts raw video or image sequences from different sources: fixed surveillance cameras, in-vehicle dash cams, cloud storage, etc. The acquisition module uses standard input formats (MP4, AVI, image folders, RTSP streams), metadata extraction (timestamps, camera IDs, GPS) and the first round of quality checks (frame, resolution, corruption). We will apply preprocessing steps like denoising, deblurring, frame rate normalization, etc., and create logs and warnings on any anomalies if needed. Everything from the preprocessed images to the metadata is saved to disk in a common folder structure for downstream reproducibility.

\item \textbf{Extracting and Synchronizing Of Frames} \\
Extracting frames is done at user-defined intervals, either using fixed-rate or adaptive sampling strategies such as motion detection or scene change analysis. The extracted frame images are indexed and stored either in  lossless PNG or JPEG format.  The extracted frames have a json file with them that lists the extraction parameters and their timestamps.  It also specifies what frames were either dropped or interpolated. At the time of multi-camera deployment, we have a synchronization service which tries to identify the frames across each stream by timestamp matching or cross-correlation. It also ensures temporal consistency for multi-view fusion and tracking.

\item \textbf{Object Recognition and Data Sharing}\\
The object detection module takes each extracted frame as input.  It outputs a list of detected objects. Each detected object is represented as a JSON object with bounding box coordinates, class ID, confidence level and other optional attributes. The results of each detection are written to a per-frame JSON file and can be visualised on-screen as an annotated image for debugging. The detection module can process in bulk with GPU support that streams results into downstream modules via queues in memory or writes to disk for batch pipelines. The system has a detection validator that marks low-confidence or ambiguous detected frames for manual examination or reprocessing.

\item \textbf{Instance segmentation and mask propagation}\\
The instance segmentation module, Mask2Former, accepts all of the detected items. Furthermore, it generates pixel-level masks per item. Depending on downstream needs, masks are saved as binary PNGs or run-length encoded arrays/JSON polygonal contours. The segmentation module also measures the mask quality (IoU, boundary smoothness) and logs its failures (e.g. missing mask, less confidence). Object metadata also propagates masks to the next stage, while a mask tracker maintains correspondence between detections and segmentation outputs across frames.

\item \textbf{Estimating depth and fusing objects}\\
For every frame, the depth estimation module (e.g., MiDas) creates a dense depth map, which is stored as a 16-bit grayscale image or NumPy array. The mean and variance of depth values of each segmented object is computed and added to the metadata of the object. The depth maps are normalized and aligned with the segmentation masks to separate them correctly. In a setup with multiple sensors (stereo cameras and LiDAR), the depth maps of the sensors are fused using their calibration parameters. The fusion is logged, along with errors, for traceability.

\item \textbf{Tracking and managing state across modules}\\
The tracking section (for instance, DeepSORT) accepts detection, segmentation, and depth data to maintain object identity. It uses a state database (either in-memory or on-disk SQLite) to keep track of features and locations. When tracking results such as assignment matrices and confidence scores, we are able to debug and analysis afterwards. The occlusion reasoning module accepts per-frame tracking outcomes in a common JSON format. This includes results such as IDs, bounding boxes, masks, depth, and appearance embeddings.

\item \textbf{Reasoning About Occlusion, Its Forecast, and Its Mistake} \\
The occlusion reasoning module analyzes mask overlaps, depth ordering, and object trajectories to infer occlusion events. When an object is deemed occluded, its last known state and predicted trajectory are recorded along with an occluded flag and confidence scores in the metadata. The module generates polygons for occluded areas and updates visualization overlays. When errors occur, routines for handling them catch the wonky things that happen – objects coming back with new IDs, mask and depth discrepancy and more. Routines either trigger automation for recovery (reassignment of IDs, smoothing of trajectories) or frame the frame for checking. All occlusion events are stamped with the objects affected and the reasoning to audit it.

\item \textbf{Output Making, Looking at, and Data Exporting}\\
The final output is given as annotated videos (with bounding boxes, masks, depth, IDs, occlusions etc. overlays), structured JSONs (per-frame/per-object), summary report (statistics, error logs, performance metrics). The output module allows streaming of results to web dashboards, exporting them to the cloud, and integration with other systems (traffic, insurance) via a REST API or message queue. Tools that visualize help users go frame by frame, look at the intermediate results, and download raw or processed data.

\item \textbf{Both Real-Time and Batch Processing Modes}\\
The pipeline has been designed to operate either in real-time like live traffic monitoring, or in batch style for offline forensic analysis. When operating in real-time mode, the different modules base their communication on asynchronous message queues like ZeroMQ, RabbitMQ etc. The use of such message queues allows for backpressure and load balancing which effectively manages the different input data rates. Modules in batch mode work over pre-recorded data. It is possible to set a checkpoint and resume for long-running jobs. The system can automatically detect failures and recover from them on its own. Other features include logging all processing actions, and the ability to reprocess any stage and increments of any task. Further, it does so without the need to rerun the entire pipeline. 

\item \textbf{Compatibility, Extended Usability and Monitoring}\\
Data exchanges are in open versioned formats (JSON, NumPy, PNG, MP4) that validate against a schema and are backward compatible for upgrading. The system opens plug-in interfaces for new modules (like other detectors, custom analytics) and has hooks for external monitoring and alerting (Prometheus, Grafana). We collect detailed logs and metrics at each stage to allow for performance profiling, anomaly detection, and automated alerts for issues concerning system health and data integrity. To make onboarding and maintenance easier, we have documented the architecture by using data flow diagrams, API specs, and config templates.
\end{itemize}

To sum up, the architecture ensures that there is a robust, transparent and extensible operation that will work across a broad a range of deployment scenarios, ranging from a single-camera research setup through to a city-wide traffic monitoring network. Robust logging, validation, and error handling in the system ensure that the answers it produces can be trusted. As a scientist adapts to new challenges, so the system is capable of improvement and adaptation. 

\subsection{Design Rationale}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The modularity allows each stage to be upgraded or replaced separately (e.g. replacing YOLO with a new detector).
    \item The intermediate outputs are stored so that we track step by step.
    \item Our system is scalable. It can be deployed on multiple computers or GPUs.
\end{itemize}

\section{Data Preparation and Label Conversion}
High-quality and consistently formatted training data is essential for reliable detection and segmentation performance. The system uses the BDD100K dataset that consists of large-scale annotated image traffic data. However, this data is in JSON format and incompatible with YOLO. This system provides a solution to convert the JSON data into an YOLO compatible format. To address this, a conversion module was developed. \textbf{BDD100K to YOLO Conversion:} The program bdd\_to\_yolo.py turn BDD100K-style instance and box annotations into YOLO labels. The script reads for each image the bounding box coordinates $(x_1,y_1,x_2,y_2)$ and the class. We normalize coordinates to the range $[0,1]$ which assures scale invariance during training. The standard YOLO transformation equations are applied as in equations~\ref{eq:eq41}, \ref{eq:eq42}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:eq41}
x_{center} = \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2w}, \quad 
y_{center} = \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2h},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:eq42}
w_{norm} = \frac{x_2 - x_1}{w}, \quad 
h_{norm} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{h},
\end{equation}
where $w$ and $h$ are the original image width and height.
Each label is written to matching .txt file with the format as in ~\ref{eq:eq43}:  
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:eq43}
\text{class\_id} \;\; x_{center} \;\; y_{center} \;\; w_{norm} \;\; h_{norm}
\end{equation}
To speed up conversions, the script uses multiprocessing in Python in order to generate labels for each partition of dataset in parallel. The final dataset has the structure of three directories: `train`, `val`, and `test`, as expected by YOLO. It includes extra helpful features that will help in marking the incomplete annotated and corrupted images.

\noindent \textbf{Additional Preprocessing:}.
We change the size of the images and improve their quality using OpenCV and Albumentations. Strengthening the model’s generalization capacity against random lighting conditions and partial occlusions helpers, motion-blur is simulated on the actual video using external applications.

The complete video-processing pipeline executes through run video pipeline py for frames extraction detection segmentation depth estimation tracking occlusion prediction.  Each stage can be debugged, replaced, and enhanced independently because of its modular design.
\noindent \textbf{Pipeline Orchestration:}.
The script handles directory creation, logging, memory management, and sequential execution of all submodules. This helps to save intermediate and final outputs in structured folders for easy retrieval and auditing.

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Frame Extraction}\\.
OpenCV’s high-speed decoder splits input videos into separate frames. Each individual frame is captured with a timestamp and saved for deterministic processing later down the stream. Frame rates may be fixed or adaptive, based on the scene. The frames that are taken out form the basis for detection, segmentation, depth and tracking. 

\item \textbf{YOLO Detection}\\.
The file video\_pipe\_yolo.py loads our trained model and runs it on each frame. According to Detections, there are bounding boxes, confidence score, and class predictions. The system:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Screen detections with adjustable confidence level.
        \item Creates visual boxes and labels for better understanding.
        \item Saves structured JSONs of each frame containing detection metadata
    \end{itemize}
    These bounding boxes act as inputs for the stages of segmentation and tracking. 

    \item \textbf{Instance Segmentation}\\.
The file video\_pipe\_seg2.py uses Mask2Former to get the mask of an object. With the help of YOLO bounding boxes, the proposal filters segments the objects at the pixel level. A JSON file is generated for the polygonal approximations of the mask so that the shape of occlusions can be accurately analyzed.
\item \textbf{Depth Estimation}\\.
We use the MiDaS model to obtain depth information via \texttt{video\_pipe\_midas.py}. For each frame, a dense depth map $D_t$ is produced. We calculate the mean, min and max depth values of an object $o$ from its segmentation mask. To be able to reason about which object is in front of which, relative distances and occlusion of objects by other objects, depth is a necessity.

    \item \textbf{Tracking}\\.
The tracking module assigns consistent IDs to objects in consecutive frames. It integrates.
\begin{itemize}
\item YOLO detections,.
\item Deep appearance embeddings,.
\item Depth-aware Kalman filtering.
\end{itemize}
The system estimates the speed of the object and the direction of motion along with their identification even though there can be partial or temporary occlusions of the object. This ensures smooth trajectories necessary for occluded motion prediction.

\item \textbf{Occlusion Detection and Prediction}\\.
The main occlusion reasoning logic is in their advance occlusion. The system determines oclusion by comparing the masks, bounding boxes, depth values, and tracking predictions of the object.
If an object disappears, its position is predicted using equation~\ref{eq:eq44}:
    \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:eq44}
    \hat{p}_{t+1} = p_t + \mathbf{v}_o \Delta t,
    \end{equation}
   It is combined with depth continuity checks to ensure plausible motion. The expected polygons, inferred bounding boxes, and occlusion flags are stored as annotated images and structured JSON.



\item \textbf{Output Aggregation}\\.
All intermediate results including the frames with YOLO detections, the segmentation masks, the depth maps, the tracking log and occlusion prediction are saved into folders.
The master JSON file collates information about each frame.
\begin{itemize}
\item End-to-end auditability,.
\item Downstream analytics,.
You can quickly connect it to insurance and traffic analysis tools.
\end{itemize}.
We also generate annotated videos for visual inspections, where we overlay different information such as detection and segmentation, tracking IDs, depth shading, and occlusion predictions.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Algorithmic Implementation}
\subsection{YOLO Detection (video\_pipe\_yolo.py)}
The YOLO detection script loads YOLO and processes each frame independently. In every single frame, it predicts bounding boxes, class labels, and confidence scores for every detected object. Detections below a confidence threshold are filtered out. Based in the weight file and the video file, the model draws each bounding box on each frame that it detects. Further, the results are saved in a JSON file which contains the list of objects detected in a per-frame manner.  This output is the basis for all downstream processing.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{YOLOv11n Detection Pipeline}
\label{alg:yolo}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} Input directory of frames
\State \textbf{Output:} Annotated frames, per-frame detection JSON
\State Load YOLO model weights
\For{\textbf{each} frame in input directory}
    \State Run YOLO detection on frame
    \For{\textbf{each} detected object above confidence threshold}
        \State Extract bounding box, class label, confidence
        \State Draw bounding box and label on frame
        \State Add detection to per-frame object list
    \EndFor
    \State Save annotated frame
    \State Append detections to output JSON
\EndFor
\State Save output JSON file
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Instance Segmentation (video\_pipe\_seg2.py)}
All detected objects are segmented at the pixel level using Mask2Former segmentation script. We crop the region of interest for each bounding box and pass it through the Mask2Former model to get a binary mask. We take the biggest mask, resize it to fit the bounding box of the full image, and place it again in the full image. Using contour extraction and polygon approximation, the mask is then turned into a polygon. The output JSON contains a polygon (poly2d) for each object, which is key to occlusion analysis.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Mask2Former Instance Segmentation Pipeline}
\label{alg:segmentation}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} Input directory of frames, YOLO detection JSON
\State \textbf{Output:} Annotated frames, per-frame segmentation JSON
\State Load Mask2Former model and processor
\For{\textbf{each} frame in input directory}
    \State Read YOLO detections for frame
    \For{\textbf{each} YOLO bounding box}
        \State Crop region of interest (ROI) from frame
        \State Pass ROI to Mask2Former
        \State Extract all instance masks
        \State Select largest mask
        \State Resize mask to fit bounding box in full frame
        \State Place mask into full-size mask
        \State Convert mask to polygon (contour extraction, approximation)
        \State Add polygon to object list
    \EndFor
    \State Overlay masks and polygons on frame
    \State Save annotated frame
    \State Append segmentation results to output JSON
\EndFor
\State Save output JSON file
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Depth Estimation (video\_pipe\_midas.py)} The MiDaS model generates a dense depth map for every frame as the process of depth estimation. A binary mask is created from the polygon for each segmented object, for which depth statistics (mean, min, max, std) are calculated over the mask. These statistics are added to the object data. The script also draws a visual overlay showing an object depth, which provides an idea of occlusion order and spatial relationships.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{MiDaS Depth Estimation Pipeline}
\label{alg:depth}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} Input directory of frames, segmentation JSON
\State \textbf{Output:} Annotated frames, per-frame depth JSON
\State Load MiDaS model and processor
\For{\textbf{each} frame in input directory}
    \State Run MiDaS to predict dense depth map
    \For{\textbf{each} segmented object polygon}
        \State Create binary mask from polygon
        \State Compute mean, min, max, std depth in masked region
        \State Add depth statistics to object
    \EndFor
    \State Generate color overlay for depth visualization
    \State Save annotated frame
    \State Append depth results to output JSON
\EndFor
\State Save output JSON file
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Tracking (video\_pipe\_deepsort.py)}
DeepSORT tracking is enhanced with polygon and area-based logic. For each frame, detections (with polygons and depth) are associated with existing tracks using both appearance features and bounding box IoU. The tracker maintains a history of object positions and uses area overlap to help maintain ID continuity, especially during occlusion. When an object is partially occluded and its visible area drops below expected, the polygon is extended forward along its motion direction (using centroid shift and binary search) to recover the likely full extent. This extension is stored as poly2d\_adjusted. Track IDs and histories are updated for each object, and all results are saved with the frame.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{DeepSORT Tracking and Polygon Adjustment}
\label{alg:tracking}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} Input directory of frames, per-frame detection/segmentation/depth JSON
\State \textbf{Output:} Annotated frames, per-frame tracking JSON
\State Initialize DeepSORT tracker and per-track history
\For{\textbf{each} frame}
    \State Prepare detections (polygon, bbox, depth)
    \State Update DeepSORT tracker with detections and frame
    \For{\textbf{each} track}
        \State Match to detection by IoU and appearance
        \State Update track ID, history, and centroid list
        \If{visible area $<$ expected area $\times$ tolerance (0.95)}
            \State Estimate motion vector from centroid history
            \State Extend polygon forward along motion direction
            \State Store adjusted polygon as poly2d\_adjusted
        \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \State Save updated tracking info and annotated frame
\EndFor
\State Save output JSON file
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Advanced Occlusion Detection (advanced\_occlusion.py)}
This script loads DeepSORT tracking results and applies further occlusion reasoning. For each frame, it uses the AdvancedOcclusionDetector to analyze tracked objects, their polygons, and depth information. The detector identifies objects that are likely occluded by comparing positions, areas, and depth continuity. It can flag objects as occluded, extend their polygons, and update the output JSON accordingly. Visualizations are generated for each frame, showing occlusion predictions and object overlays. The final output includes updated JSON and annotated frames which will be recombined into a video file.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Advanced Occlusion Detection and Visualization}
\label{alg:occlusion}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} Input directory of frames, DeepSORT tracking JSON
\State \textbf{Output:} Annotated frames, per-frame occlusion JSON
\State Load DeepSORT tracking results
\For{\textbf{each} frame}
    \For{\textbf{each} tracked object}
        \State Analyze polygon, area, and depth
        \State Compare with other objects for overlap and depth ordering
        \If{object is likely occluded (overlap with nearer object)}
            \State Mark as occluded
            \State Optionally extend polygon using poly2d\_adjusted
        \EndIf
        \State Update object status and polygon
    \EndFor
    \State Generate visualization overlays
    \State Save annotated frame
    \State Append occlusion results to output JSON
\EndFor
\State Save output JSON file
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

	model = YOLO('runs/detect/train7/weights/best.pt')
	aggregated_data = {
		"frames": []
	}

	if conf < confidence_threshold:
	continue  
	class_id = int(box.cls[0])

	x1, y1, x2, y2 = map(float, box.xyxy[0])
	img = draw_minimal_bbox(img, x1, y1, x2, y2, class_name)
	obj = {
		"category": class_name,
		"id": idx,
		"box2d": {
			"x1": x1,
			"y1": y1,
			"x2": x2,
			"y2": y2
		},
		"confidence": float(box.conf[0])
	}
	objects.append(obj)
	out_frame_path = os.path.join(output_dir, frame_file)
	cv2.imwrite(out_frame_path, img)
	timestamp = int((frame_idx / fps) * 1000)

		"objects": objects
	}

    }

        yolo_objects = yolo_data["frames"][frame_idx]["objects"]
        objects = []

        for obj_id, yolo_obj in enumerate(yolo_objects):
            category = yolo_obj["category"]
            bbox = yolo_obj["box2d"]
            x1, y1, x2, y2 = map(int, [bbox["x1"], bbox["y1"], bbox["x2"], bbox["y2"]])

            roi_width, roi_height = roi.size

                inputs = processor(images=roi, return_tensors="pt").to(device)
                outputs = model(**inputs)
                target_sizes = [(roi_height, roi_width)]

                segmentation = results["segmentation"].cpu().numpy()
                segments_info = results["segments_info"]

                best_mask = None
                best_area = 0
                for seg in segments_info:
                    mask = (segmentation == seg["id"]).astype(np.uint8)
                    area = mask.sum()
                    if area > best_area:
                        best_area = area
                        best_mask = mask

                if best_mask is not None:
                    mask_resized = cv2.resize(best_mask, (x2 - x1, y2 - y1), interpolation=cv2.INTER_NEAREST)
                    full_mask = np.zeros((img_height, img_width), dtype=np.uint8)
                    full_mask[y1:y2, x1:x2][mask_resized > 0] = 1
                    kernel = np.ones((3, 3), np.uint8)
                    full_mask = cv2.morphologyEx(full_mask, cv2.MORPH_OPEN, kernel)
                    polygon = mask_to_polygon(full_mask)
                        obj = {
                            "category": category,
                            "id": obj_id,
                            "attributes": yolo_obj["attributes"],
                            "poly2d": polygon,
                            "confidence": yolo_obj.get("confidence", None)

                'depth': obj.get('depth', {'mean': 0})
            })
            
        track_ids = list(prev_state_by_tid.keys())
        det_to_tid = {}  # Detection index -> track ID
        
        if track_ids and detections:
            C = np.full((len(track_ids), len(detections)), 1e6, dtype=np.float32)
            for i, tid in enumerate(track_ids):
                prev_state = prev_state_by_tid[tid]
                pred_bbox = predict_bbox(prev_state, motion_hist_by_tid.get(tid, deque()))
                
                for j, det in enumerate(detections):
                    iou = bbox_iou(pred_bbox, det['bbox'])
                    depth_diff = abs(prev_state['depth'] - det['depth']['mean'])
                    C[i,j] = 1.0 - iou + 0.1 * depth_diff
            
            row_ind, col_ind = linear_sum_assignment(C)
            for i, j in zip(row_ind, col_ind):
                if C[i,j] < 0.7:
                    det_to_tid[j] = track_ids[i]
        
        for j, obj in enumerate(frame['objects']):
            tid = det_to_tid.get(j, next_fallback_track_id)
            if tid == next_fallback_track_id:
                next_fallback_track_id += 1
            
            obj.update({
                'track_id': tid,
                'occluded': j not in det_to_tid,

            })

                poly = None

            if tid not in motion_hist_by_tid:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption=Advanced Occlusion Detection]
def calculate_iou(self, poly1, poly2):
	intersection = self.poly_intersection_area(poly1, poly2)
	x1, y1 = zip(*[(p[0], p[1]) for p in poly1])
	area1 = self.poly_area(x1, y1)
	x2, y2 = zip(*[(p[0], p[1]) for p in poly2])
	area2 = self.poly_area(x2, y2)
	union = area1 + area2 - intersection
	return intersection / (union + 1e-6)
\end{lstlisting}

\section*{Correct Predictions (Diagonal Elements)}

The diagonal terms in the confusion matrix refer to cases where the predicted class and the actual class match. The performance of the model changes across different classes.

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Car:} The model achieves exceptional performance for the 'car' class with \textbf{75,929} correct predictions, representing the highest true positive count across all classes. The system enables detection of objects and vehicles, which is vital for occlusion detection.
	
	\item \textbf{Traffic Signs}: Model gets it right for 21,348 instances, quite a remarkable feat with regards to static road infrastructure.
	
	\item \textbf{Traffic Light:} \textbf{15,077} instances of traffic lights are correctly classified, indicating reliable detection of traffic control devices.
	
	\item \textbf{Person:} The model correctly identifies \textbf{8,185} instances of pedestrians, which is essential for comprehensive scene understanding.
	
	\item \textbf{Truck:} \textbf{2,401} truck instances are correctly classified, showing reasonable performance for larger commercial vehicles.
	
	\item \textbf{Bus:} \textbf{875} bus instances are correctly identified, with some confusion observed with other vehicle classes.
	
	\item \textbf{Bike:} \textbf{501} bicycle instances are correctly classified, though the relatively low count suggests potential challenges with smaller objects.
	
	\item \textbf{Rider:} \textbf{267} rider instances are correctly identified, with notable confusion observed with the 'person' class.
	
	\item \textbf{Motor:} Only \textbf{198} motorcycle instances are correctly classified, indicating challenges with detecting this class.
	
	\item \textbf{Train:} The model is showing a lot of struggle with train detection since it has only predicted \textbf{1} correctly. This indicates that the class is possibly underrepresented in the training data. Alternatively, this class might be posing unique challenges.
	
	\item \textbf{Background:} The model has \textbf{0} predictions for the background class, which shows that there is a problem with classifying the background.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Major Wrong Classifications}

\subsection*{Background Class Confusion}

One of the biggest issues related to classification is the ‘background’ class, which confuses across pairs both ways:

\textbf{False Positives (Background misclassified as objects):}
\begin{itemize}
	\item 25,878 true background instances wrongly predicted as ‘car’.
	\item 13,296 real traffic signs incorrectly predicted as "'traffic sign".
	\item 11,626 background images wrongly predicted as traffic light.
	\item 4,943 true background instances misclassified as 'person'.
	\item 962 true background instances are misclassified as truck.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{False Negatives (Objects misclassified as background):}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Over 13000 instances of car were misclassified as background.
	\item 7,178 true 'traffic sign' instances mispredicted as background
	\item 3,953 instances of true traffic light wrongly classified as background.
	\item 2,196 'person' instances were mistakenly detected as 'background'.
	\item 1,050 true 'truck' instances misclassified as background.
\end{itemize}

This bidirectional confusion means that the model seems to be confused between background region and objects. This can be due to ambiguity in boundaries, similarity of visual features or class imbalance.

\subsection*{Inter-Class Confusions}

Several classes that mean the same grow confused of each other:

\begin{itemize}
\item There are 99 times where a ‘person’ is predicted incorrectly as a ‘rider’, and 53 times where ‘rider’ is predicted as a ‘person’. It’s to be expected given the likeness of the riders and pedestrians visually.
	
	\item The confusion between traffic signs and traffic lights means that instances of traffic lights on the dataset are identified wrongly as traffic signs, and vice versa. Traffic control systems are represented by both classes.

	\item There is some confusion between vehicle types in the class:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item 154 times cars were wrongly identified as buses.
		\item The word 'car' is actually 'bus' 96 times
		\item 526 'trucks' incorrectly classified as 'cars' in result.
	\end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\section*{Performance Summary}

The confusion matrix reveals several key insights:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item The model is performing well for "car" which is the most important class among others for vehicular occlusion detection.

	
	\item The biggest challenge is the model fails to classify the background class at all (0 correct predictions) and lots of confusion going both ways with the object classes.

	
	\item There are different performance results for different classes. This probably indicates there's a class imbalance problem in the training data set. Certainly, car class is over represented whereas train, motor classes are under represented.

	
	\item The model is understandably confused between classes that are nearly similar in meaning such as person and rider or traffic sign and traffic light. Getting it to resolve these classes may require additional data collection or engineering of features.

	
	\item The detection rates for smaller classes (i.e., bike, rider, and motor) were lower indicating there could be scale variation-related challenges with the dataset.

\end{enumerate}

\section*{Recommendations for Model Improvement}

The following recommendations were arrived at based on the confusion matrix analysis.

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Background Class Refinement:} Implement targeted data augmentation and training strategies specifically for the background class to improve its discrimination from object classes.
	
	\item We must counter class imbalance by weighted loss functions, data augmentation, and resampling strategies. Focus on underrepresented classes, such as train and motor.
	
	\item \textbf{Multi-Scale Training:} Enhance detection capabilities for smaller objects by incorporating multi-scale training and feature pyramid networks.
	
	\item \textbf{Contextual Features:} Leverage contextual information to better distinguish between semantically similar classes (e.g., person vs. rider, traffic sign vs. traffic light).
	
	\item \textbf{Post-Processing:} Implement post-processing techniques such as non-maximum suppression refinement and confidence thresholding to reduce false positives, particularly for background misclassifications.
\end{itemize}
